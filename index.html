Fluidity

Fluidity is a series of artworks in the topic of the suppressed emotions of women.
As a woman, particularly as an East Asian woman, I have consistently felt and observed this nuanced atmosphere. The women in my life, ranging from my grandmother to my friends, all show a certain level of intricate restraint. We face the oppressive weight of societal norms, endure gender exploitation, and are expected to make sacrifices for the sake of family and fertility. Consequently, our mental well-being carries an underlying element of pathology, yet nearly all of us choose to bear it silently. We refrain from emotional outbursts, prioritizing the care of others' feelings, and assume a facade of normalcy out of apprehension regarding societal scrutiny. It's a complex mixture of emotions — pain, disappointment, anger, despair, all masked by the cover of calmness. We are muted, fading away in silence. However, we are resilient; within the slience and suppression, we extend open arms to embrace and protect the things we love.

This series contains two artworks. One is a 3D-modeling work to show the suppressed emotions, named Frozen. The other work, Silent Scream is an interactive work to show the different expressions of emotions.

Meditation

Meditation is a series of interactive artworks related to brain-computer interaction (BCI) and more general human-computer interaction (HCI). It reflects my contemplation on the concept of "thinking."
Since the summer of 2022 when I started to know about BCI/HCI, I have been curious about the connection between the physiological and spiritual dimensions of "thinking." The term "thinking" seems to encompass various fields, from academic disciplines like neuroscience, psychology and philosophy, to casual discussions in everyday life. People have been interpreting, deconstructing, and thinking of "thinking." And I also wish to contribute my own reflections.

This series contains artworks basically based on TouchDesigner, To Think, Neuro, Light House, and Fractal Garden.

To Think | The More We Think, The More...

This is an abstract piece that translates brainwave activity into mathematical geometric shapes. Each instance of user focus (thought) adds a new edge to the geometric shape, and the central brightness and clarity of the shape increase with the user's higher brainwave intensity. Ultimately, this shape will approach a circle infinitely.
Due to the abstract nature of the work, it allows for multiple interpretations. I personally offer two interpretations varying significantly.
One suggests that as we think more, our thoughts continually improve, approaching the perfection of a circle. However, infinite proximity does not equate to attainment; we can never reach perfection, so continuous thinking is necessary, and the more we think, the more we gain.
The other interpretation suggests that as we think more, our ideas become more complex, but overly complex thoughts eventually become indistinguishable from zero (a circle). Therefore, the more we think, the less we gain.
Hence, the answer to the question "The more we think, the more we...?" is not singular, and I use "..." to represent it. Everyone can attribute specific meaning to the "..." and I am also curious about others' interpretations.

Data Collection & Input: OpenBCI GUI
Visualization: TouchDesigner

Example:

TouchDesigner: Here I used the OSC streaming data of EEG signals collected by OpenBCI GUI as the input.
User's brainwave activities and intensity will influence the edge and the brightness of the geometric shape.
Keyboard input is also added to simulate the detection of changes in EEG patterns in case the user does not have BCI equipment at hand.

Neuro | Brain Activity Simulation

This is an interactive work that simulate brainwave activity with mouse input. If the discharge of neurons is considered as the rational, indifferent, and mechanized interpretation of "thinking" in neuroscience — a deconstruction of the concept of "thinking," then simulating neuronal discharge with a machine represents the computer's rational, indifferent, and mechanized interpretation of neuroscience — an additional deconstruction of the deconstruction of "thinking."
As the user moves the mouse, the trajectory, speed, and dwell time of mouse movement are recorded. Then, dots with varying intensities visualize the movement trajectory based on the recorded speed and dwell time. The recording concludes when the dwell time exceeds 5 seconds.

Data Input: Mouse Gestures
Visualization: TouchDesigner

Example:

TouchDesigner: Here I used the mouse gestures as the input.
The mouse gestures are mathematically transformed from uv into xyz for the visualization. The trajectory, speed, and dwell time of mouse movement will influence the intensity and the trajectory of the dot visualization.

Light House | Mind Wandering

This is a metaphorical art piece. I see meditation itself as a light house. As you wander around the vast universe of your thoughts, whether exploring or feeling lost, it offers you light. You don't necessarily have to move directly towards it; you just know it's there.
It uses mouse input to direct the movement of the particles. This process is to simulate mind wandering.

Data Input: Mouse Gestures
Visualization: TouchDesigner

Example:

TouchDesigner: Here I used the mouse gestures as the input.
It uses mouse input to direct the movement of the particles. As the user moves the mouse, particles will move and rotate towards the direction of the mouse.

Fractal Garden | In the Universe of Nature and Mathematics

This is an interactive piece that combines CircuitPython with TouchDesigner to create a growing fractal tree. The fractal expands, twists, and breathes in response to temperature input, while a galaxy-like particle background also shifts according to real-time data.
I offer two main interpretations. One sees the work as a metaphor for human growth: as our inner state changes, our environment and connections also evolve, reflecting a balance of expansion and limitation. The other interpretation focuses on the tension between control and surrender: our body influences the fractal and galaxy, yet the patterns remind us that we are equally shaped by forces beyond ourselves.

Data Collection & Input: CircuitPython temperature sensor
Visualization: TouchDesigner

Finale | Pull-down-style Comic

It is a pull-down-style comic with the topic of student suicide phenomena in East Asia.
It is not a rare case to see the headlines report student suicides. Even in my high school, there will be one or two students jumping off the building or overdosing on sleeping pills every year. What makes it even more horrific is that people are gradually regarding these incidents as normal. Instead of questioning the reason behind student suicides, people just say ruefully: “another one”. All the tears and sorrows, all the struggling and potentials, are compressed into a simple number, “one”. So I make an attempt to explain the student suicide and fight against this kind of indifference.
It is a short story showing a fleeting memory of a girl who committed suicide. By the illustration of the girl and her delusional self, the factors behind her suicide are implied. Also, in this comic, I tried to discuss the uncanny space of home and school, which are the two places where we students spend most of our time in. Love from family members and friendship with schoolmates are sacred. We tend to be comfortable and relaxed if we fit in well. While it will be extremely painful if we feel excluded and be outsiders to the places we should familiar with.

Hand drawing
Post-processing: Adobe Illustrator

Mapping Light: Tide | Wander Between Reality and Reflection

This animation, created with Blender and TouchDesigner, visualizes the cyclical beauty of sunrise and sunset over the ocean.
Two parallel scenes—day and night—mirror one another through variations in color, depth, and motion. The daytime scene drifts gently on calm waves, while the night scene conveys a quiet unease, as if sinking beneath the surface. Together, they evoke the emotional rhythm between serenity and disquiet, capturing the fluid boundary between imagination and reality.

Visualization: Blender + TouchDesigner
Audio: Dual soundscapes — natural and slowed seagull calls

Solar

Solar is a series of artworks with the theme of the sun. It involves religious and cultural elements of Shamanism in northern China, Buddhism in Tibet and solar culture in Egypt and Janpan as well as my personal impressions.

Thangka | The Wheel of Life, The Wheel of Sun

Thangka is a traditional form of painting in Tibet. It is deeply influenced by Tibetan Buddhism and is typically employed to depict scenes from the Buddhist context.
In this artwork, I began by hand-drawing Bhavachakra, the Wheel of Life, including sketching and ontlining in gold. Following that, I performed post-processing in Photoshop to enhance the circular elements.

Hand drawing: Sketch & Outline in gold
Post-processing: Adobe Photoshop

Fragments

It is a series of 25 icon/symbol desgins in topic of the sun.
These designs are influenced by Shamanic, Egyptian and Japanese cultures, featuring patterns and symbols associated with the sun. For example, I used scattered dots and divergent lines, which represent the sun in Shamanism, Egyptian cultural elements like pyramids, scarabs, and talismans, as well as Japanese symbols such as the three-legged crow and bronze mirrors.

Tool used: Adobe Illustrator

Slices of the Sun

This 3D extension of the Fragments series transforms 2D sun symbols into spatial structures. Each model represents a “slice” of the sun, composed of layered geometric forms that reinterpret traditional solar symbols through the lens of digital fabrication.
Many of the shapes are inspired by depictions of the sun in Shamanic, Egyptian, and Japanese cultures, while others are newly imagined to express the act of dissection and reconstruction. The creative process felt like anatomizing the sun—turning light and myth into tangible specimens.

Tool used: Houdini

Solar Owl

Extending from the Fragments 3D series, this set of five models combines the geometric “sun slices” with a scanned 3D owl model, creating hybrid forms that merge symbolic representations of light and perception.
Each piece embodies the coexistence of illumination and observation—the sun as the eternal source of energy, and the owl as its nocturnal witness. Through layering, fusion, and reconstruction in Houdini.

Tool used: Houdini, 3D Scanner, 3D Printer


Rhythm of the Sun

This motion piece is a logo animation inspired by Manchu shamanic sun symbols. It combines geometric precision with rhythmic motion to convey the energy and sacredness of the sun. Through cyclical expansion, contraction, and pulsation, the animation translates the symbolic clarity of traditional motifs into a contemporary digital expression of solar vitality.

Tool used: Adobe Illustrator & After Effects

Divine Reflection | Panorama

This panoramic image merges natural and spiritual motifs into a meditative landscape. A monumental figure emerges from the trees, blending human, divine, and natural forms into a seamless continuum.
By using equirectangular projection, the work invites viewers to step into a 360° vision where the sacred and the organic coexist, suggesting a cosmic reflection that transcends physical perspective.

Tool used: Adobe Photoshop & 360° panoramic rendering (equirectangular)

BCI Application Projetcs

Welcome to my BCI application project page. This page serves as a showcase of research projects during my undergraduation eduation in the field of brain-computer interface.

There are mainly two projects:
One is a student summer research project in 2022, focusing on controling movements and directions of objects on screen based on visual motor imagery.
The other is my signature work, aiming to build a classification model to analyze EEG signals and detect the 4 states of human: hunger, thirst, feeling cold and feeling hot.

2022 Student Summer Research

It is a team-based research program. I was responsible for:
Literature reviews on the brain structures, EEG signals, and tutorials of OpenBCI.
Experimental design and implement.
Data collection and pre-processing.
Design of the presentation slides and presentation poster.

View the team presentation slides: Team Presentation View the team research report: Team Report

Signature Work

It is also a team-based program. I was responsible for:
Literature reviews on cognitive circuits, brain activity measurement methods and references of classification model building.
Experimental design and implement.
Data collection and pre-processing.
Collaborated in model training and testing with application of a hybrid network structure.


We have recruited and collected 168 sets of biological data from around 60 undergraduate students.
The current classification model reached an accuracy of 91.9% in training sets and 71.7% in testing sets.

About Me

I am Zhiyu (Arya) Zhou, a graduate student in the MA program in Digital Art History / Computational Media at Duke University. With a background in mathematics, I approach digital media through both technical and artistic perspectives.
My creative work spans Brain-Computer Interface (BCI) research, interactive installations, digital drawing, 3D modeling, and motion graphics. I am particularly interested in how immersive technologies and computational tools can reshape narrative, perception, and artistic expression.
